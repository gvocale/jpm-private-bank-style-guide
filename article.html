Primal and Futuristic

by Julia Peyton-Jones and Hans-Ulrich Obri

Directors, Serpentine Galleries

JAN 14, 2015

Download PDF

Every year, the Serpentine Galleries, a mecca for contemporary art at the center of London’s Kensington Gardens, commissions a Pavilion by an architect who hasn’t yet designed a building in the United Kingdom.

Some of the most renowned names in architecture have created these temporary structures, built and open for just three months, including Frank Gehry, Kazuyo Sejima and Ryue Nishizawa, and Zaha Hadid, who designed the first Pavilion in 2000. Beginning two years ago, however, the visionaries behind the Pavilion series, co-curators Julia Peyton-Jones and Hans-Ulrich Obrist, began looking beyond the big names to identify highly gifted architects such as Sou Fujimoto of Japan, who designed last year’s Pavilion, and this year, Smiljan Radic, one of the stars of Chile’s architectural renaissance.

For this year’s Pavilion, Radic created a cylindrical structure sitting on large quarry stones that is both primal and futuristic. “While enigmatically archaic, in the tradition of romantic follies, Radic’s design for the Pavilion also looks excitingly futuristic, appearing like an alien space pod that has come to rest on a Neolithic site,” says Ms.Peyton-Jones.

Contrasting qualities

Radić’s experimentation with natural materials such as rock and wood first attracted the curators’ attention at the Venice Biennale in 2010. “We were very impressed,” explains Mr. Obrist, “with how Radić uses materials in a physical way, which in the age of the internet seems important. It’s very tactile—you can touch it.”The original model for the Pavilion was made out of papier-mâché and masking tape, and Radić wanted the finished design to have contrasting qualities of smooth and rough, fragile and tough, translucent and opaque. “This is something I work for in a lot of projects,” explains Radić. “There is the sensation of really thin skin inside—fragile and translucent, and then from the outside, the sensation of opaque and weathering.” Radić achieved these contrasts by layering fiberglass sheets over a doughnut-shaped structure that has been likened to a cocoon. It appears opaque from the outside, yet from the inside it is translucent and open to views of the lawn, the sky and the Serpentine Galleries nearby.

“Radić is a master of bringing air into the space. He didn’t want to do something suffocating because, obviously, that would make no sense here in the park, where we are in open air,” says Mr. Obrist. “So he created this ring structure, and within that there are openings and a lot of air to breathe.” In this way, Radić’s design also evokes the use of follies in the 18thcentury English landscape garden. “The follies,” explains Radić, “give you the sensation of extravagant ambiance” because they introduce the exotic or unexpected, the structures often being Japanese or Grecian. And they are also typically in a state of ruin, where “nature is making its way into the building in a special way.” “So we have this closed volume,” continues Radić, “almost like a tortoise shape, but we destroy it in some places to have a really close relation with the landscape.”

A friendly space

Consisting of passages that lead to a central space and café, the structure also manages to create a sense of retreat and protection. Explains Radić, “I care to have people who are inside feel the space in a special way—to feel they are in a calm and friendly space.”

Radić’s innovative design—as well as those of the Pavilion architects preceding him—is the result of having in essence a “blank canvas” on which to work. Ms. Peyton-Jones explains, “What is a great freedom for the architects is that they don’t have design kitchens or lavatories. It’s not a space about practicalities. It’s a space where we encourage them to press their architectural language to the greatest degree, and we support them in that process.”
